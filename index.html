Classification

Introduction to Taxonomy

Taxonomy is the science of classifying living organisms into groups based on their characteristics. Scientists organize life into categories such as domain, kingdom, phylum, class, order, family, genus, and species. This system helps make sense of the huge diversity of life on Earth.

By grouping organisms based on shared traits, taxonomy allows scientists to identify relationships between species. It also helps with naming organisms using a universal system called binomial nomenclature, so scientists around the world can communicate clearly.

Monera Kingdom

The Monera kingdom includes all prokaryotic organisms, which means their cells do not have a nucleus. Bacteria are the main members of this kingdom, and they are usually microscopic and found almost everywhere on Earth.

These organisms reproduce quickly and can survive in extreme environments. Some bacteria are helpful, such as those in your gut, while others can cause diseases. Their simple structure makes them very efficient and adaptable.

Protist & Fungi Kingdom

Protists are a diverse group of mostly single-celled organisms that live in moist environments. Some protists, like algae, can make their own food through photosynthesis, while others must consume food like animals.

Fungi are different because they are multicellular and absorb nutrients from their surroundings. They play an important role as decomposers, breaking down dead organisms and recycling nutrients back into ecosystems.

Animal Kingdom

Animals are multicellular organisms that must consume food for energy. They have specialized cells and tissues that allow them to move, respond to their environment, and carry out complex processes.

The animal kingdom is divided into many groups based on characteristics such as body structure and symmetry. Animals play important roles in ecosystems, including as predators, prey, and pollinators.

Body Systems

Circulatory System

The circulatory system is responsible for transporting blood, oxygen, nutrients, and waste throughout the body. The heart pumps blood through a network of blood vessels including arteries, veins, and capillaries.

This system works closely with others, like the respiratory system, to deliver oxygen to cells and remove carbon dioxide. Without circulation, cells would not get the materials they need to survive.

Respiratory System

The respiratory system allows the body to take in oxygen and remove carbon dioxide. Air enters through the nose or mouth, travels down the trachea, and reaches the lungs where gas exchange occurs.

Inside the lungs, tiny air sacs called alveoli allow oxygen to pass into the blood while carbon dioxide leaves the blood. This process is essential for cellular respiration and energy production.

Digestive System

The digestive system breaks down food into smaller molecules that the body can absorb and use. This process begins in the mouth and continues through the stomach and intestines.

Enzymes and acids help break down food, while nutrients are absorbed into the bloodstream. Waste products are then eliminated from the body, keeping the system balanced.

Integumentary & Skeletal

The integumentary system includes the skin, hair, and nails, and acts as the body's first line of defense. It protects against injury, infection, and helps regulate body temperature.

The skeletal system provides structure, support, and protection for internal organs. It also works with muscles to allow movement and produces blood cells in the bone marrow.

Muscular System

The muscular system allows the body to move and maintain posture. Muscles contract and relax to create movement, and they are controlled by signals from the nervous system.

There are three types of muscle: skeletal, smooth, and cardiac. Each type has a specific function, from moving limbs to controlling internal organs and pumping blood.

Immune / Excretory / Endocrine

The immune system protects the body from harmful pathogens like bacteria and viruses. It uses cells and proteins to identify and destroy invaders.

The excretory system removes waste from the body, while the endocrine system releases hormones that regulate growth, metabolism, and other processes. Together, they help maintain balance in the body.

Nervous System

The nervous system controls and coordinates all body activities. It includes the brain, spinal cord, and nerves, which send signals throughout the body.

This system allows you to respond to stimuli, think, learn, and remember. It works quickly to process information and maintain communication between different parts of the body.
